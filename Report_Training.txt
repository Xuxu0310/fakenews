4.1 BiLSTM-Attention Model
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

4.1.1 Model Architecture

The Bidirectional Long Short-Term Memory network with attention mechanism (BiLSTM-Attention) is chosen for its ability to capture sequential dependencies and long-range patterns in text data through gating mechanisms that address the vanishing gradient problem. The bidirectional variant processes input in both forward and backward directions, capturing contextual information from past and future tokens simultaneously. The incorporated attention mechanism further enhances the model by computing importance weights for each position in the sequence, allowing the network to focus on the most relevant portions of text while potentially ignoring less critical background information.

The attention pooling layer is implemented as follows:

```python
class AttentionPooling(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, hidden_dim):
        super().__init__()
        self.proj = nn.Linear(hidden_dim * 2, hidden_dim)
        self.score = nn.Linear(hidden_dim, 1, bias=False)

    def forward(self, outputs, mask):
        energy = torch.tanh(self.proj(outputs))
        scores = self.score(energy).squeeze(-1)
        scores = scores.masked_fill(~mask, -1e9)
        weights = torch.softmax(scores, dim=1)
        context = torch.bmm(weights.unsqueeze(1), outputs).squeeze(1)
        return context, weights
```

This mechanism computes attention scores for each position in the sequence, applies masking to ignore padding tokens, and produces a weighted context vector that emphasizes the most important words for the classification decision. The attention weights can be examined to understand which words contributed most to the prediction, providing model interpretability that is valuable for understanding what the model has learned.

The BiLSTM classifier is implemented as follows:

```python
class BiLSTMAttentionClassifier(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, vocab_size, embed_dim, hidden_dim, num_layers, dropout, pad_idx=0):
        super().__init__()
        self.pad_idx = pad_idx
        self.embedding = nn.Embedding(vocab_size, embed_dim, padding_idx=pad_idx)
        self.embedding_dropout = nn.Dropout(dropout)
        self.lstm = nn.LSTM(
            input_size=embed_dim,
            hidden_size=hidden_dim,
            num_layers=num_layers,
            batch_first=True,
            bidirectional=True,
            dropout=dropout if num_layers > 1 else 0.0
        )
        self.attention = AttentionPooling(hidden_dim)
        self.norm = nn.LayerNorm(hidden_dim * 2)
        self.classifier = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Dropout(dropout),
            nn.Linear(hidden_dim * 2, hidden_dim),
            nn.ReLU(),
            nn.Dropout(dropout),
            nn.Linear(hidden_dim, 1)
        )
```

The model employs a learnable embedding layer that maps vocabulary words to 200-dimensional vectors, serving as a bridge between discrete text tokens and continuous neural network inputs. The classification components consist of fully connected layers that compress features to 128 units with ReLU activation, introduce dropout regularization of 0.35 to prevent overfitting, and produce a single logit value passed through a sigmoid function for the final probability output.

The training methodology employs Binary Cross-Entropy (BCE) loss as the primary optimization target. This loss function measures the cross-entropy between predicted probabilities and true binary labels, penalizing confident but incorrect predictions more heavily than uncertain incorrect ones. The AdamW optimizer is selected for training due to its adaptive learning rate capabilities and built-in L2 regularization through weight decay. Early stopping with a patience of 3 epochs is implemented to monitor the validation loss and halt training when overfitting is detected, ensuring that the model state with the best validation performance is restored rather than the potentially overfitted final state.

4.1.4 Learning Rate Ablation

The learning rate is a critical hyperparameter that controls the step size at which the model's weights are updated during gradient descent optimization. Too high a learning rate can cause the model to diverge or oscillate around the optimal solution, while too low a learning rate results in painfully slow convergence and potential underfitting. To identify the optimal learning rate for the BiLSTM-Attention model, a systematic ablation study was conducted with four different learning rates: 0.0001, 0.001, 0.01, and 0.1, keeping all other hyperparameters constant.

The experimental results reveal a clear performance pattern across different learning rates for the BiLSTM-Attention model. The learning rate of 0.0001 results in the lowest performance with a best test accuracy of approximately 65%, indicating that the model is underfitting due to the slow learning speed. The model fails to converge to an optimal solution within 6 training epochs, and both training and validation accuracies remain significantly below their potential maximum. This suggests that a larger number of epochs or a higher learning rate is necessary for this setting.

The learning rate of 0.001 achieves the best performance with a best test accuracy of approximately 75% and a final test F1 score of approximately 67%. The training loss decreases smoothly, and the validation accuracy shows steady improvement across epochs before plateauing. This represents the optimal balance between learning speed and stability for this particular dataset and model architecture, allowing the model to converge effectively without diverging.

The learning rate of 0.01 shows faster initial convergence with training loss dropping rapidly, but the model exhibits signs of overfitting with the validation accuracy beginning to decline after epoch 3 while the training accuracy continues to increase. The test accuracy peaks at approximately 73% but is lower than the 0.001 setting, suggesting that faster learning causes the model to specialize too quickly on the training set without generalizing effectively to the validation and test sets.

The learning rate of 0.1 demonstrates severe overfitting behavior, with the training loss reaching near-zero values while the validation and test losses remain high and the test accuracy dropping to approximately 60%. The model essentially memorizes the training data but fails to generalize to unseen data. This extreme behavior highlights the importance of selecting an appropriate learning rate that balances convergence speed with generalization capability. Based on these findings, the learning rate of 0.001 is selected as the optimal configuration for the BiLSTM-Attention model.

4.1.5 Batch Size Ablation

The batch size determines how many training examples are processed together in each iteration of gradient descent optimization. A smaller batch size provides more frequent weight updates but introduces more gradient noise, potentially affecting training stability. A larger batch size provides more stable gradient estimates but fewer weight updates per epoch and may require more memory. To investigate the impact of batch size on model performance, a systematic ablation study was conducted with five different batch sizes: 8, 16, 32, 64, and 128, keeping all other hyperparameters constant.

The experimental results for the BiLSTM-Attention model demonstrate the impact of batch size on training stability and final model performance. The batch size of 8 results in the lowest performance with a best test accuracy of approximately 68%, and the training curves exhibit considerable instability with frequent fluctuations in both training and validation accuracy. The small batch size introduces excessive gradient noise that prevents the model from converging smoothly, and the final model state is suboptimal even with early stopping enabled.

The batch sizes of 16 and 64 achieve moderate performance with best test accuracies of approximately 72% and 73% respectively. Both configurations show improved stability compared to the batch size of 8, with smoother training curves and more consistent validation performance. However, neither reaches the performance levels achieved by intermediate batch sizes, suggesting that there is a trade-off between batch size and training stability that depends on the specific characteristics of the dataset.

The batch sizes of 32 and 128 achieve the best performance with best test accuracies of approximately 74% and 72% respectively. The batch size of 32 shows particularly stable training with smooth convergence and consistent improvement across epochs, reaching its peak performance around epoch 3 before the validation loss begins to plateau. The batch size of 128 achieves comparable performance with slightly faster training times per epoch, making it a viable alternative when memory constraints are not a concern. Based on these findings, the batch size of 32 is selected as the default configuration for its optimal balance of stability and performance for the BiLSTM-Attention model.

4.2 TextCNN Model

4.2.1 Model Architecture

The Text Convolutional Neural Network (TextCNN) represents a fundamentally different approach to processing text data. Rather than treating text as a sequential structure like the LSTM, the CNN views text as a two-dimensional representation where one dimension corresponds to the sequence of tokens and the other to the word embeddings. The TextCNN applies multiple convolutional filters of varying kernel sizes across the text sequence, with each filter learning to detect specific local patterns or n-grams.

The TextCNN classifier provides an alternative architecture with parallel processing:

```python
class TextCNNClassifier(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, vocab_size, embed_dim, num_filters=128, kernel_sizes=(3, 4, 5), dropout=0.35, pad_idx=0):
        super().__init__()
        self.embedding = nn.Embedding(vocab_size, embed_dim, padding_idx=pad_idx)
        self.convs = nn.ModuleList([
            nn.Conv1d(embed_dim, num_filters, kernel_size=k) for k in kernel_sizes
        ])
        self.dropout = nn.Dropout(dropout)
        self.fc = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(num_filters * len(kernel_sizes), num_filters),
            nn.ReLU(),
            nn.Dropout(dropout),
            nn.Linear(num_filters, 1)
        )
```

This architecture applies three parallel convolutional filters with kernel sizes of 3, 4, and 5, allowing the model to capture n-grams at multiple granularities simultaneously. Each filter produces feature maps that undergo max pooling to extract the most salient features, which are then concatenated and passed through fully connected layers for classification. This design processes all positions in the sequence in parallel, making it computationally more efficient than sequential approaches.

The TextCNN model employs the same embedding layer as the BiLSTM-Attention model, mapping vocabulary words to 200-dimensional vectors. The fully connected layers follow a similar structure with dropout regularization of 0.35 and a final sigmoid activation function, ensuring architectural compatibility for fair comparison between the two approaches.

4.3 Architecture Comparison and Discussion
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The final comparative experiment evaluates the performance difference between BiLSTM-Attention and TextCNN architectures using the optimal hyperparameters identified from previous ablation studies (learning rate of 0.001, batch size of 32, BCE loss). This experiment aims to determine whether the additional complexity and sequential processing capabilities of the BiLSTM-Attention model yield meaningful performance improvements over the simpler and faster TextCNN architecture, or whether the CNN approach is sufficient for this particular fake news detection task.

[Insert Figure: architecture_comparison.png showing two training curves comparing BiLSTM-Attention and TextCNN with different colors]

The experimental results reveal that both architectures achieve nearly identical performance on this task. The BiLSTM-Attention model achieves a best test accuracy of approximately 74.1% with a final test F1 score of approximately 62.1%. In comparison, the TextCNN model achieves a best test accuracy of approximately 74.5% with a final test F1 score of approximately 63.5%, representing a slight but consistent advantage across both accuracy and F1 score metrics. This parity in performance suggests that for this particular dataset and task, the simpler and faster CNN architecture is sufficient and does not require the additional complexity of the BiLSTM-Attention model.

A critical difference emerges in training efficiency. The TextCNN model trains approximately 6.5 times faster than the BiLSTM-Attention model, with each epoch requiring approximately 1.2 seconds compared to 7.8 seconds for the LSTM. This dramatic speed advantage stems from the CNN's ability to process all positions in the sequence in parallel rather than sequentially, eliminating the recurrent dependencies that require sequential processing. For practical applications where training time or computational resources are constraints, the TextCNN architecture represents a more efficient choice without sacrificing performance.

The overfitting analysis provides another point of comparison. The BiLSTM-Attention model exhibits a larger gap between training accuracy (89%) and test accuracy (74.1%), representing a 15 percentage point overfitting. In contrast, the TextCNN model shows a smaller gap between training accuracy (85%) and test accuracy (74.5%), representing an 11 percentage point overfitting. This difference suggests that the BiLSTM-Attention model may be learning training-specific patterns more aggressively, potentially due to its higher capacity and sequential processing that can learn long-range dependencies but may also learn spurious patterns specific to the training set.

Based on the experimental results, the TextCNN architecture is recommended for this fake news detection task. The recommended choice is based on three key factors: equivalent or slightly superior accuracy and F1 score, significantly faster training speed, and a smaller overfitting gap indicating better generalization capability. The BiLSTM-Attention model remains valuable for scenarios where interpretability through attention weights or capture of long-range dependencies is particularly important, but for the current dataset and evaluation criteria, the efficiency advantage of the TextCNN makes it the preferred choice.
