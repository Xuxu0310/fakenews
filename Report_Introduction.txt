1. INTRODUCTION
===============================================================================


In digital age, rapid proliferation of information through social media platforms has fundamentally transformed how people consume news. While this democratization of information sharing offers numerous benefits, it has also created fertile ground for the spread of misinformation and fake news. Fake news, defined as deliberately fabricated or misleading information presented as legitimate news, has emerged as a serious societal problem with far-reaching consequences. The ease with which false information can be created and shared, combined with algorithmic recommendation systems that prioritize engagement over accuracy, has enabled fake news to spread at unprecedented speeds and scales. This phenomenon not only causes public confusion and erodes trust in legitimate news sources but can also influence political outcomes, damage reputations, and incite social unrest.

Traditional approaches to combating fake news have relied heavily on manual fact-checking by human experts and journalists. While human fact-checking remains essential, it is fundamentally limited by its scalability and speed. The sheer volume of content generated daily across social media platforms far exceeds what human fact-checkers can process manually. Moreover, by the time a claim is verified and debunked, false information has often already reached millions of users and caused significant damage. This temporal gap between misinformation spread and verification presents a critical vulnerability in our information ecosystem.

The emergence of machine learning and deep learning techniques offers a promising path toward automated fake news detection systems that can scale to meet this challenge. By training models on large collections of labeled news articles, these systems can learn to identify patterns and features that distinguish authentic news from fabricated content. Such automated systems offer several advantages over manual approaches: they can process millions of articles in real-time, provide consistent and unbiased judgments, assist human moderators by flagging suspicious content, and potentially intercept misinformation before it goes viral. The development of effective automated detection tools is not merely an academic exercise but a practical necessity for maintaining the integrity of information in democratic societies.

The motivation for this project stems from both the urgency of the fake news problem and the opportunity to apply state-of-the-art deep learning techniques to address it. Social media platforms struggle to balance the need for rapid content moderation with the protection of free speech, and automated detection systems can provide crucial support to human moderators. News aggregators and recommendation engines can incorporate fake news detection scores to promote reliable sources while demoting potentially misleading content. Furthermore, individual users can benefit from browser extensions or applications that warn them when they encounter articles with characteristics of fake news.

This project has several primary objectives. First, it seeks to build a complete binary classification system for fake news detection using deep learning approaches. Second, it implements and compares two different neural network architectures—a Bidirectional LSTM with Attention mechanism and a Text Convolutional Neural Network (TextCNN)—to evaluate their relative effectiveness on this task. Third, the project conducts comprehensive hyperparameter ablation studies, systematically exploring the impact of different loss functions, learning rates, and batch sizes on model performance. Finally, it evaluates models using a suite of metrics to provide a thorough assessment of model behavior and identify the optimal configuration.

The expected outcomes of this project include a well-documented, modular codebase that can serve as a foundation for further research, trained model checkpoints that achieve reasonable accuracy on fake news classification, and comprehensive experimental results that provide insights into what factors most influence model performance. By systematically comparing architectures, loss functions, and hyperparameters, this work aims to identify best practices and optimal configurations for fake news detection using deep learning. The insights gained from this analysis can inform both researchers developing more sophisticated detection systems and practitioners deploying such systems in real-world applications.

The remainder of this report is organized as follows. Section 2 describes the problem formulation, including the task definition, research questions, and main challenges. Section 3 details the data preprocessing pipeline and dataset characteristics. Section 4 presents the model architectures, loss functions, and training methodology. Section 5 describes the evaluation protocol and metrics. Section 6 presents comprehensive experimental results and analysis. Section 7 discusses the findings, limitations, and potential improvements. Sections 8 and 9 conclude with a summary of contributions and directions for future work.
